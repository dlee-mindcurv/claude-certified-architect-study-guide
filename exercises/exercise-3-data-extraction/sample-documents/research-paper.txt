Title: Longitudinal Effects of Remote Work on Employee Productivity and Well-Being

Authors: Dr. Maria Santos (Stanford University),
         Dr. James Park (MIT Sloan School of Management),
         Dr. Aisha Patel (London School of Economics)

Published: Journal of Organizational Behavior, Vol 52, Issue 3, March 2025
DOI: 10.1002/job.2025.0312
Received: October 15, 2024
Accepted: January 28, 2025

Abstract:
This longitudinal study tracked 8,200 knowledge workers across 45 companies
over a 30-month period (January 2022 - June 2024) to assess the sustained
effects of remote and hybrid work arrangements on productivity metrics and
employee well-being indicators. Our findings reveal that hybrid workers
(2-3 days remote per week) showed 12% higher productivity as measured by
output-per-hour compared to fully in-office peers (p < 0.001), while fully
remote workers showed 7% higher productivity (p < 0.01). Well-being scores,
measured via the WHO-5 index, were 15% higher for hybrid workers and 9%
higher for fully remote workers compared to in-office controls. However,
career advancement rates for fully remote workers were 23% lower than
in-office peers over the study period, suggesting a visibility penalty.
Hybrid workers showed no statistically significant difference in advancement
rates. These effects were consistent across industries, company sizes, and
geographic regions, with minor variations by age cohort.

Keywords: remote work, hybrid work, productivity, well-being, career advancement,
          longitudinal study

Sample Size: 8,200 participants
Companies: 45
Duration: 30 months
Methodology: Mixed-methods longitudinal cohort study with quarterly surveys
             and objective productivity metrics from company HR systems
